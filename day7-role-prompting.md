# My Claude Usage Strategy

**Your profile:** Professional · Research & Learning focus · Daily use · Wants Fast Answers most often

---

## Recommended Primary Model
**Claude Sonnet 5**

## Why This Model Fits Me
- You're a **daily user** doing **research/learning** — you need something that's sharp and fast enough to not feel like a chore to open every day.
- You said **Fast Answers** matter most — Sonnet 5 is Anthropic's best balance of speed and reasoning quality, and it's what most people should default to unless a task specifically demands more raw horsepower or less cost.
- Opus (currently **Opus 4.8**) is the "go deep" model — better for gnarly multi-step reasoning, but slower and pricier. For everyday research questions, that's overkill.
- Haiku (**Haiku 4.5**) is built for speed/cost at scale — great for quick lookups, but noticeably less capable for nuanced research synthesis.

---

## When to Use Haiku
- Quick factual lookups, definitions, or summarizing a short article
- Rephrasing/formatting text
- High-volume, low-stakes tasks where speed >> depth

## When to Use Sonnet
- **Your daily driver.** Research questions, learning new concepts, explaining complex topics, drafting notes/summaries, comparing ideas
- Anything where you want a thoughtful, well-reasoned answer *quickly*

## When to Use Opus
- Multi-layered research synthesis (e.g., "compare these 5 competing theories and identify the weakest assumptions in each")
- Long, ambiguous, high-stakes reasoning chains
- When Sonnet's answer feels shallow or you're not fully convinced by its logic

---

## Recommended Effort Level

Claude models now support an **effort** setting (Low / Medium / High / Max) that controls how much "thinking" Claude does before answering — independent of which model you pick. Higher effort = more thorough but slower/costlier.

### Low
Quick clarifications, simple lookups, casual back-and-forth where speed matters more than depth.

### Standard (Medium)
**Your everyday setting.** Good for most research and learning questions — solid reasoning without excessive latency.

### High
Reserve for research questions with real nuance — synthesizing multiple sources, evaluating tradeoffs, or when you plan to *act* on the answer (e.g., a decision that affects your work).

### Max
Rare — save for genuinely hard, high-stakes problems where you want Claude to fully reason through edge cases before responding.

## My Personalized Claude Workflow

| Task | Best Model | Best Effort | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|
| Quick fact-check / definition | Haiku 4.5 | Low | Speed matters, low complexity |
| Daily research reading & summarizing | Sonnet 5 | Standard | Best speed/depth balance |
| Learning a new concept, step-by-step | Sonnet 5 | Standard–High | Needs clear reasoning, not just facts |
| Comparing competing frameworks/theories | Sonnet 5 or Opus 4.8 | High | Requires weighing tradeoffs carefully |
| High-stakes research report or decision-critical analysis | Opus 4.8 | High–Max | Depth and rigor matter more than speed here |
| Brainstorming / exploratory questions | Sonnet 5 | Low–Standard | Fast iteration matters more than polish |

---

## Biggest Mistakes I Should Avoid
1. **Defaulting to Opus for everything** — you'll pay in speed and cost for depth you often don't need.
2. **Leaving effort on Max out of habit** — slows down simple daily questions for no real benefit.
3. **Using Haiku for real research synthesis** — fine for lookups, but it'll flatten nuance you actually care about.
4. **Not escalating when it matters** — if a research answer will influence a real decision, bump to Sonnet/High or Opus rather than accepting the first fast answer.

---

## Final Recommendation
**If you could use only ONE model + ONE effort level for most of your work:**

> **Claude Sonnet 5 at Standard effort.**

It matches your daily-use pattern, handles research and learning tasks with strong reasoning, and stays fast enough that it won't feel like friction to reach for every day. Reserve Opus/High-Max for the handful of times a question is genuinely hard or consequential enough to justify the extra time and cost.
